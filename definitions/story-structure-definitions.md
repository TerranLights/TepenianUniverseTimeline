# Story Structure — Fused Beat Definitions

> **Provenance:** sourced from `TerranLights/CurrentNovelDocs/reference/story-structure-definitions.md` — the universe-agnostic beat-structure framework used across all novels in this line. Maintained in sync with that repo. For how these beats apply specifically to this series' per-book structure, see `series-overview/themes-and-structure.md`.

This is a composite story-beat structure fusing four sources, each brought in for the specific purpose it serves — not one primary framework with the others layered on top:

*   **Blake Snyder, _Save the Cat_** — standard beat names and the recommended duration percentages; the baseline structural skeleton the other sources attach to. Drawn from as fully as possible.
*   **James Scott Bell, _Plot and Structure_** — the "Doorway of No Return" framing for the two act breaks, the "Mirror Moment" for the midpoint, and supplementary beats (Kick in the Shins, Care Package, Q-Factor, Pet the Dog). Drawn from as fully as possible.
*   **John Truby, _The Anatomy of Story_** — character-web concepts (Ally/Allies, Opposition, Fake Ally, Moral Decision, Self-Revelation, etc.). Drawn from as fully as possible.
*   **Joseph Campbell, _The Hero's Journey_** — used selectively, not comprehensively: a Campbell beat is kept only if it can be reinterpreted into non-fantasy (e.g. sci-fi) form, or if it cross-references cleanly onto a Save the Cat beat (Road of Trials = Fun and Games; Refusal of the Call = Debate). Beats that are irreducibly fantasy-coded and don't reinterpret cleanly — e.g. **Magic Flight** — are deliberately left out.

## Beat-by-Beat Definitions

### Opening Image

_Snyder._ The first visual/scene of the story — a thematic or grabbing image, scene, or short sequence that establishes tone, mood, and scope. Functions as the "before" snapshot of the hero (or world) that will have transformed by the story's end.

### Theme Stated

_Snyder._ At ~5% — halfway into Set-Up — another character directly, though not blatantly, instructs the protagonist in their own psychological need, delivered as a localized, specific context rather than an abstract statement of theme. Example: a child wants a brand-new bicycle, and wants it immediately; at the 5% mark, the child's father tells him "you need to be patient" — a concrete, situational line that Act 3 later turns out to have been about all along.

### Set-Up

_Snyder._ The opening ~10%: establishes the hero's ordinary world, weaknesses, and goals before the Catalyst disrupts it.

### Care Package

_Bell. Placement: somewhere within Set-Up._ Some form of gift given to the protagonist — not a literal "birthday present" or "Christmas present," but a form of goodwill. Can be physical, verbal, psychological, or "actional" (something someone does). This is the setup half of a payoff pair with Bell's **Q-Factor** (see Dark Night of the Soul) — the two are explicitly built together.

### Catalyst

_Snyder / Bell ("Disturbance") / Campbell ("Call to Adventure")._ The actual triggering event. Important craft rule: this is the _one and only_ event in the entire story allowed to be random/coincidental — every other event must follow causally from what came before. Because it's the sole exception, it's especially important that this particular event actually matters.

### Debate

_Snyder / Bell ("Argument Against Transformation") / Campbell ("Refusal of the Call")._ The hero's hesitation before committing to the journey — "Can I face this challenge? Do I have what it takes? Should I go at all?" The last chance to say no. These three terms name the same beat, not merely a thematically similar one.

### Break into Two

_Snyder / Bell ("Doorway of No Return #1")._ The hero commits and enters the new world of the story — literally (leaves home, takes the case, joins the mission) or internally (an irreversible decision, accepting a truth). Should read as a door closing behind them; the old rules no longer apply.

### B-Story

_Snyder. Placement: starts early in Act 2 (Road of Trials), converges with the A-plot at the Midpoint._ A parallel plot — a separate plotline that _seemingly_ has no connection whatsoever to the main story, which then converges with the main story at the Midpoint/Mirror Moment. The "seemingly unrelated" quality is load-bearing: the disconnection from the A-plot must read as genuine to the audience right up until the convergence, not be telegraphed early.

### Kick in the Shins

_Bell. Placement: early Act 2._ An event of things going wrong for the protagonist. No exact percentage marker — only that it should happen early in Act 2 ("you shouldn't let things stay trouble-free for very long"). Must be internally consistent with the surrounding world and relevant to the plot, not a random, disconnected setback.

### Road of Trials / Fun and Games / Promise of the Premise

_Campbell / Snyder._ The part of the story that delivers on the premise's hook — whatever made the audience want this story in the first place actually gets experienced here (the magic school, the investigation, the romance). These two terms name the same beat, not merely overlapping concepts. Typically the longest single beat in the story (20–50%).

### Pet the Dog

_Bell. Placement: close to the Midpoint, just before or after._ The protagonist temporarily departs/sidesteps from the main plot to help another character who is weaker than themselves. This action must have an effect (direct or indirect) on the progression of the rest of the plot, and must contribute somehow to the course of events leading up to the triggering of Act 3.

### Midpoint / Mirror Moment

_Snyder / Bell._ Snyder: either a **False Victory** or a **False Defeat**. Bell's Mirror Moment: the protagonist confronts three realization-questions — _Who am I? What have I become? What must I become?_ — or realizes _"I'm probably going to die,"_ where "death" can be **physical** (literal death), **social** (ostracism, loss of reputation/respect, a partner leaving), **professional** (losing a job they're committed to), or **psychological** (realizing a belief/belief-system held was not just wrong but falsely life-supporting). Campbell's Hero's Journey has no clearly identified equivalent "middle" beat.

### Self-Revelation

_Truby. Placement: flexible, anywhere across the entire second half of Act 2 (Midpoint through Break into Three); default preference is to place it at All Is Lost, but not a hard rule — place it wherever a given story's own logic calls for it._ The hero has a major revelation about who they really are, and grows as a person as a result — unless the revelation is so painful it destroys them instead. Two variants: a **psychological** self-revelation (stripping away a self-facade, seeing themselves honestly for the first time) or a **moral** self-revelation (realizing they've been wrong and have hurt others, and must change). Self-Revelation is the end-point of the same arc that "Need" begins back in Act 1.

### Bad Guys Close In

_Snyder._ The second half of Act 2 — obstacles compound and become overwhelming, the protagonist's usual strengths stop working, plans get foiled, allies/teams may fracture. Pressure builds on both an external axis (literal antagonists, rivals, systems, deadlines) and an internal/psychological axis (the protagonist's own flaws finally catching up with them) simultaneously — widely considered the hardest beat to write well.

### All Is Lost

_Snyder._ The low point — the protagonist's situation collapses, no hope visible. Traditionally paired with a **"whiff of death"**: a real or symbolic death (a mentor, an object, an idea) signaling that the old self/old way of thinking is dying, bridging into Dark Night of the Soul.

### Dark Night of the Soul

_Snyder._ The darkness before the dawn — the hero wallows in absolute hopelessness, beaten and _knowing_ it, before picking themselves up and digging deep for a solution. Often the point where the story's theme reflects most directly back at the protagonist. Can be very brief — as short as a single beat/moment. Both Truby's **Moral Decision** and Bell's **Q-Factor** land somewhere within this span.

### Moral Decision

_Truby. Placement: within Dark Night of the Soul._ The protagonist is forced to choose between two (or more) equally difficult, equally plausible moral choices — constructed so the choice is directly related to the protagonist's own **Moral/Character Flaw**, established back in Act 1's Setup. Explicitly paired with Truby's own **Self-Revelation** and Bell's **Q-Factor** — all three used together.

### Q-Factor

_Bell. Placement: within Dark Night of the Soul; payoff half of the Care Package/Q-Factor pair._ During a period of great strife and hardship, the protagonist remembers that they received the Care Package — physically, verbally, through something in the environment, or simply through memory. Acting on this renewed mindset, the protagonist becomes able to act decisively.

### Break into Three

_Snyder / Bell ("Doorway of No Return #2")._ The moment of clarity — the hero finally understands what they need to do, and more importantly, who they need to become to do it. This is where the A-story and B-story intertwine to help the hero find the solution; often the B-story's own lesson "clicks into place" here, giving the hero what they need to try again.

### Finale — the "Five-Point Finale" / "Storming the Castle"

_Snyder._ The final ~20% of the story, broken into five sequential steps: (1) **Gather the Team**, (2) **Storm the Castle**, (3) **High-Tower Surprise**, (4) **Dig Down Deep**, (5) **Executing the New Plan**. Applies across genres, not just action/adventure — every finale shares the common thread of pursuing a concrete goal.

### Closing Image

_Snyder._ Structurally the mirror/bookend of the Opening Image: the "after" snapshot of the hero/world, showing how much has changed since the opening. Should visually or thematically echo the Opening Image, ideally in contrast — same setup, transformed outcome.

## Character-Web Concepts (Truby)

Unlike the Save the Cat/Bell beats above, Truby's character-web concepts aren't tied to a specific point on a percentage timeline — they describe character _relationships and functions_, applicable wherever the relevant characters interact.

### Fake Ally

Does **not** mean "traitor" or "backstabber." A Fake Ally is a character established as an ally to the protagonist in Act 1, who later acts _against_ the protagonist — but does so in a way fully aligned with the Fake Ally's own belief system, goals, objectives, hesitations, fears, and principles, not out of betrayal. Example: the protagonist wants to eat at a spicy-food restaurant; their ally has a severe intestinal condition that makes spicy food unbearably painful, and steers them toward a non-spicy restaurant instead. The ally never betrays or backstabs the protagonist at any point — they're simply acting in their own genuine interest, which happens to conflict with the protagonist's.

### Ally / Allies

Self-explanatory: the ally (or allies) to the protagonist.

### Opposition

Self-explanatory: whatever/whoever constitutes the opposition to the protagonist.

## Campbell Concepts (used selectively, placement flexible)

These beats are kept only if they can be reinterpreted into non-fantasy form (per the source-list rule above). Unlike the Save the Cat/Bell beats, placement along a story's percentage timeline is not fixed — apply wherever fits a given story's specific events, rather than anchored to one percentage range.

### Meeting with the Goddess

The hero encounters a powerful embodiment of love or goodness, and must earn a genuine connection with this figure. Through it, the hero discovers how boundless and powerful love and unconditional surrender can be.

### Atonement with the Father

The hero confronts and is initiated by whatever holds ultimate power in their life — often a father figure or a god-like/authority figure — and must reconcile with that power to move forward. Frequently involves the hero realizing this figure is mortal and flawed, and may have to rely on this imperfect being for help regardless.

### Apotheosis

A period of rest, peace, and fulfillment following the hero's trials, before their return. The old self dies and a new, more enlightened self is born. Doesn't necessarily mean literal godhood — sometimes it indicates the hero has transcended their former mental or physical state into something more sophisticated.

### Deliberately excluded

**Magic Flight** — irreducibly fantasy-coded, does not reinterpret cleanly into non-fantasy form. Any other Hero's Journey beat not listed above is likewise not currently part of this fused framework; add one only if it can be reinterpreted the same way.
