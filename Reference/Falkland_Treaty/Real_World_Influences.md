# Falkland Treaty — Real-World Influences, Running Record

**Purpose:** a living log of which real-world treaties actually ended up shaping the Falkland Treaty, and specifically what each one contributed — kept separate from `Reference/Materials/Treaties/INDEX.md` (which catalogs everything *available*, not what was actually *used*). Update this file every time a new influence is adopted, revised, or dropped during drafting. Started 2026-07-08, alongside the first structural scaffold.

**Status:** first full draft complete, 2026-07-08 — see `Falkland_Treaty_Draft_v1.md` in this same folder. Not locked canon. Everything below informed the *structure and mechanisms* first, then the actual prose.

## Corrected premise, established 2026-07-08

The Falkland Treaty is **not** a cooperative, mutual agreement between equal parties. Per `World_History_Reference.md`: Upper Earth won the War of Upper Earth; the treaty was "negotiated as an alternative" to exterminating the robot population; it was "the terms of their defeat, not a mutual compromise." This reclassifies the treaty from the Antarctic-Treaty genre (cooperative science/governance among consenting equals) to the Versailles/New-Echota genre (a settlement imposed by a victor on a defeated, exiled population, dressed in negotiated-agreement language).

**Signatories, confirmed 2026-07-08:** the future-era equivalent of the UN (representing Upper Earth collectively) against high-ranking robot leaders negotiating on behalf of their people. The robots had genuine political organization and named leadership — this was not a unilateral decree imposed on a scattered population, even though the negotiation happened from a position of certain defeat.

**Confirmed robot signatory, 2026-07-09 (previously tentative as of 2026-07-08): Maggie Aarden.** See `Worldspace/Characters/Dolls/Still-Present_-_In-Game/Maggie Aarden/README.md`. **Confirmed circumstances:** she was not a volunteer in any real sense — she was "invited" to "volunteer," the community and its human allies having already decided on her before she was ever asked. Already-established character profile: SP 5w6 ("The Castle"), an industrial yard worker and Steampunk artist from Midwestland who became "a very unwilling leader" of early robot resettlement out of loyalty rather than desire, after rising anti-robot sentiment cost her her job. Her prior solution to that same threat — quietly resettling into separate parallel communities within Upper Earth rather than fighting or submitting — previews the Falkland Treaty's exile arrangement almost exactly, just at national rather than regional scale, and her own coerced signing repeats the exact same pattern that made her a leader in the first place: unable to refuse once duty, not ambition, was what was actually being asked of her. This resolves two of her own long-standing open questions ("Did Maggie survive to the Falkland Treaty? Was she exiled to Antarctica?") and gives the preamble's New-Echota-style manufactured legitimacy a real psychological grounding: a leader pragmatic and minimal by nature, not defiant, negotiating from necessity rather than principle.

**Tone, confirmed 2026-07-08 (developer's own words):** "a balancing of 'one-sided/victor's language' and more 'neutral tone' (as it is essentially negotiating with the robots to all choose between being exiled or being essentially destroyed)." The treaty should read as a genuine negotiation offering a genuine choice, while the actual choice was never real.

**Naming origin, confirmed 2026-07-08:** the treaty is named for its actual signing location — the city of Stanley, Falkland Islands. Not a placeholder or metaphorical name.

**The United Assembly of Nations, confirmed 2026-07-08:** proposed name for Upper Earth's "future equivalent of the UN," accepted and built upon by the developer. Membership is effectively universal — the meaningful list is the short one of exclusions, not members: a handful of small nations with negligible international presence and no robot populations of their own (real-world examples given: Guam, Papua New Guinea, St. Kitts & Nevis, Tuvalu, Seychelles, Fiji, and similar). The treaty's own preamble names only a handful of confirmed Upper Earth nations as illustrative examples, not as the complete membership — as of the same day, that list is: Unified Korea, the Sinian Federation, the Sovereign Republic of Hawaii, Midwestland, the Colorado Republic, the People's Democratic Republic of Cascadia, the Appalachia Republic, the Prairie Federation, the Republic of Sonora, the Republic of Alaska, and the People's Democratic Republic of New England.

**Confirmed against an existing reference map, 2026-07-08:** `Reference/Images/Maps/North America with tentative labels.jpg` shows the full post-2083 fragmentation of the former United States (and parts of Canada) into named successor polities, the map itself using shorter tentative labels for some: Alaska (now confirmed as the full **Republic of Alaska**), the People's Democratic Republic of Cascadia, Colorado, Sonora, the Prairie Federation, the Midwest Republic (colloquially "Midwestland"), Greenland, Quebec, a DMZ strip near the Great Lakes, New England (now confirmed as the full **People's Democratic Republic of New England**), Appalachia, and a southern polity currently labeled "the CSA." Eight of these are now confirmed Falkland Treaty signatories. **Deliberately excluded for now:** "the CSA" — the developer flagged this name specifically may change before it's locked. Greenland and Quebec were not addressed either way and remain open. The DMZ is not a nation and has no signatory status.

**Scope, confirmed 2026-07-08:** the Falkland Treaty does not concern itself with what kind of government the exiled population sets up. That belongs entirely to the Tepenian Constitution, a separate document for a later time. The treaty founds Tepenia as a sovereign entity and sets the terms of exile; it does not dictate internal governmental form, the same way Versailles didn't dictate Germany's own internal constitution.

**Human-ally personhood, confirmed 2026-07-08:** humans are not stripped of legal personhood for being human — only for the specific, voluntary act of emigrating with the robot population to Tepenia. Upper Earth's legal apparatus then categorizes that human as having "willingly and permanently forfeited their right to legal personhood," and that person is barred from returning. Drafted into the treaty as Article IV.2, deliberately in the same voluntarist register as the rest of the New-Echota-style framing: the forfeiture is characterized as the human's own free choice and its natural consequence, not a punishment — even though the practical effect (permanent loss of legal personhood, barred return) mirrors what happens to robots under Article IV.1, reached by a different legal mechanism (forfeiture-by-choice rather than categorical revocation).

## Confirmed influences and what each contributed

### Treaty of New Echota (1835) — the single most load-bearing tonal model
The real treaty that manufactured its own legitimacy exactly the way the Falkland Treaty needs to. Its preamble doesn't read as a victor's decree — it reads as the Cherokee's *own* request ("the Cherokees are anxious to make some arrangements... with a view to reuniting their people... where they can establish and enjoy a government of their choice"), and it narrates its own signatories into apparent full national authority even though the real signatories were a minority "Treaty Party," not the elected Cherokee government. **Contribution:** the Falkland Treaty's preamble should similarly narrate the robot leadership's mandate as cleaner and more complete than it may have actually been — leaving room for exactly the "why the evacuation happened" ambiguity already established as a Veilkeeper mystery hook, without stating the gap outright. Also contributed: granting genuine self-governance in the new territory as part of the deal, and a reciprocal-sounding "perpetual peace and friendship" clause despite obvious power asymmetry.

### Treaty of Versailles (1919)
**Contribution 1:** Part I of Versailles is literally the entire Covenant of the League of Nations — a full charter for a new international institution nested as the opening section of a punitive peace treaty. This is the direct precedent for leading the Falkland Treaty with founding the Federation of Tepenia as a governing entity, before territorial/population provisions. **Contribution 2:** extreme concrete specificity (named towns, named rivers, exact figures) as the actual house style of real treaties, rather than vague deferrals to "a later process." **Contribution 3:** the preamble's flat, procedural, unemotional recitation of how the war started — a model for the Falkland Treaty's own preamble voice, written entirely in the victors' register.

### Lateran Treaty (1929)
**Contribution 1:** mutual-dignity language ("consistent with justice and with the dignity of both High Contracting Parties") deployed by the stronger party even while the weaker party (the Holy See) accepts a drastically diminished territorial outcome as final settlement — a real template for the "balance of victor's/neutral tone" the developer asked for. **Contribution 2:** sovereignty over a new territory established cleanly in a handful of articles with an annexed boundary map — a template for however the treaty formally vests Tepenia with sovereignty over Antarctica. **Contribution 3:** the Financial Convention — the *stronger* party pays the *weaker* party compensation as a face-saving gesture, the reverse of typical reparations — a candidate mechanism if Upper Earth owes Tepenia some kind of resource/technology transfer as part of the settlement.

### The Antarctic Treaty (1959)
**Contribution 1:** Article IV's sovereignty freeze (no claim strengthened, none renounced, none newly asserted) as the likely mechanism explaining how real, overlapping Upper Earth national claims to Antarctic territory got folded into one exile settlement without first resolving whose claim was valid — **corrected 2026-07-08: this is not about Tepenia's own internal government or capital.** Tepenia had a real historical national capital (Fort McMurdo, through the Long Night War) and has its own government, structure still undecided — that's Tepenian Constitution territory, a deliberately separate future document, not something the Falkland Treaty concerns itself with at all. Present-day Tepenia's capital-lessness is a post-war condition, not a treaty-founding one. **Contribution 2:** Article V's nuclear ban as a candidate explanation for why Tepenia's civilization runs on the Amundsen Tower grid-regulation model instead of nuclear power. **Contribution 3:** Article VII's inspection/transparency regime as a founding norm significant enough that its later violation (someone building something in secret) could be the actual first crack in the Second Interwar Period's stability, feeding into the Long Night War.

### UN Charter (1945)
**Contribution 1:** the multi-organ institutional split (General Assembly vs. Security Council vs. ICJ vs. Secretariat) as a template for splitting Jeju-do-style functions into distinct bodies within Tepenia's own governing structure — a policy/allocation organ separate from a judicial one. **Contribution 2:** Chapter VIII's Regional Arrangements clause (local disputes handled by regional bodies before escalating centrally) as the treaty-level justification for why Tepenia's six subnets function as semi-autonomous governance units. **Contribution 3:** the Trusteeship System (temporary administration with a built-in path to self-government) as real-world precedent for the Tri-Cities' already-established arc: legal separation → de facto amalgamation → full unification.

### Locarno Pact (1925)
**Contribution 1:** a tiered escalation ladder for disputes (judicial decision for rights questions → conciliation commission for everything else → central council only if conciliation fails → collective response triggers only after a formal finding) as a richer model than the Antarctic Treaty's flatter consult-then-ICJ approach. **Contribution 2:** named, explicit exceptions to a non-aggression clause (self-defense, collective action, action against a first-mover aggressor) rather than an absolute ban — giving the eventual war a specific loophole to have been exploited through. **Contribution 3:** Article 8's built-in obsolescence clause — the treaty automatically expiring once a broader system is judged sufficient — as a candidate explanation for why the Falkland Treaty never got superseded as originally planned: the war interrupted whatever evolution it anticipated.

### Partial Nuclear Test Ban Treaty (1963)
**Contribution:** Article IV's "supreme interest" withdrawal clause — any party can legally withdraw if "extraordinary events... have jeopardized the supreme interests of its country," on short notice. A candidate mechanism for how the Long Night War could begin: not through an external villain or hidden conspiracy, but through a founding nation legally invoking a clause the treaty always guaranteed it.

### Camp David Accords (1978)
**Contribution:** a phased-transition structure with explicit named timelines (three months to conclude, two-to-three years to implement, a five-year transitional authority) — a candidate template if any part of the Falkland Treaty needs its own internal implementation schedule distinct from its signing date.

## Considered but not yet adopted

- **Peace of Westphalia (1648)** — the "perpetual oblivion" mutual-amnesty clause (a formal agreement to legally forget prior hostilities) was discussed as a candidate for the preamble, contingent on whether the pre-exile founding nations carried real conflict among themselves. Not yet confirmed as applicable.
- **Oslo Accords, Sykes-Picot, Balfour Declaration, League of Nations Mandate for Palestine, Svalbard Treaty, Potsdam Agreement** — flagged as high-relevance in `Reference/Materials/Treaties/INDEX.md` (Categories 2–3) but not yet read in depth as of this file's creation. Revisit if the scaffold's remaining open questions (exact signatory-authority language, any residual Upper Earth intervention rights over Tepenia) need more real-world grounding.

If an influence gets dropped or overridden by a better idea mid-draft, move it to "Considered but not yet adopted" (or a new "Rejected" section) rather than deleting the entry — the record should show the path taken, not just the destination.
