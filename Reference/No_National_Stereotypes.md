# No National Stereotypes — A Binding Worldbuilding Rule

**Status:** Confirmed, binding — applies across every Tepenian Universe project (games, novels, TV)

**The rule:** a Tepenian city's real-world founding nation (which country originally established the underlying Antarctic research station) is a GPS coordinate only. It locates the city on the map. It must never be used as a reason, cause, or explanation for anything else in the fiction — not a city's present-day culture, not a faction's internal conflict, not a character's temperament, not which real-world city makes a good creative inspiration, not why a war strike hit one city and not another.

**Self-check:** if a sentence's meaning would change were the founding nation swapped for a different one, that sentence violates this rule.

## Every way this has been violated before (and corrected, in InnerTepeniaGDD)

1. Explaining a city's present-day culture or a faction's internal conflict via founding-nation stereotype ("German rigor," "Russian dusha") instead of the city's own independently-developed identity.
2. A bare "Being [Nationality]." or "[Nationality] Tepenian city" identity claim in a summary line.
3. Picking, rejecting, or even neutrally mentioning whether a real-world creative-inspiration pick shares a nationality with a city's founding nation — even a defensive "no overlap, no issue" note is itself a violation.
4. Framing an established in-world cultural practice (an institution, cuisine, music, art) as inherited from or carried over from the founding real-world nation, rather than developed independently by the exile community. A practice may echo a real-world tradition as coincidence; it must never be explained by that tradition.
5. Using a city's founding nation as an explanatory factor in an in-world strategic, military, or narrative decision (e.g. "Upper Earth struck City X, not City Y, because X's founding nation gave it cleaner symbolic legibility as a target").

## How to apply

- The founding nation can still appear as a bare fact: demographic tier tables, "the station was founded by X in 19XX," a naming choice honoring a specific historical figure. It just can never be the *reason* something else in the fiction is true.
- When writing a city's culture, institutions, cuisine, music, or art, build it from the exile community's own circumstances (isolation, density, terrain, founding-era pressures) — never from the founding nation's real-world culture.
- When choosing real-world creative inspirations for a city, judge them entirely on structural/thematic parallel — never mention whether they share or don't share a nationality with the city's founding nation, in either direction (even a defensive disclaimer that there's no overlap is itself the mistake).
- When explaining a plot event (why a strike hit one city and not another, why a faction formed where it did), build the explanation from the city's own established facts — population, geography, infrastructure, civic role, timeline — never from its founding nation.

## Case study: Juan Carlos

Juan Carlos (Palmer subnet) was founded on a pre-exile Spanish research station. Its own source material once framed its defining civic institution (a "tertulia") and its cuisine/music/arts as directly inherited from Spain — corrected in InnerTepeniaGDD to note any such institution would have to be independently developed by the exile community, with real-world echoes noted only as coincidence, never as cause. This repo's own Juan Carlos entry (`Worldspace/Locations/Juan_Carlos.md`) goes a step further and omits the unconfirmed cultural institution entirely rather than porting it over in reframed form, pending real independent development.
