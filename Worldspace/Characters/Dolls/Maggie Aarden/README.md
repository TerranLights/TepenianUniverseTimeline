## Personality & Voice
- Character summary: Analytical, self-contained, fiercely private, and deeply reluctant to be needed. She wanted her art, her craft, and her interior world; instead she was given a community to protect, and did it anyway — not joyfully, not naturally, but because her own sense of duty would not let her look away from people who had nowhere else to turn. Her love of Steampunk art is the character in miniature: an appreciation for intricate systems that are elegant and self-contained, where everything has a purpose and the beauty is in how the parts fit together. She understood machines; she built art from the aesthetics of machines; and then she was asked to manage people, who are neither intricate nor self-contained.
- Speech patterns / quirks / recurring phrases: (placeholder — not yet established in source)
- Behavior under stress: An SP-dominant type who builds security by minimizing her own needs, protecting her private space, and hoarding her resources (time, knowledge, energy) from the demands of the outside world — she would rather be left alone with her expertise and her inner world than be responsible for anyone else. A pull of loyalty and duty consistently overrides this instinct when a community she cares about is at stake — she cannot fully abandon people who need her, even when every part of her wants to.
- Moral tendencies: Pragmatic rather than idealistic — when facing a threat, her instinct is to withdraw, build separate space, and minimize contact with the source of the threat, rather than confront it directly.

## Backstory Summary
Maggie Aarden was from Midwestland; her recorded history begins shortly after the ruling by the International Court of Diplomacy at Jeju-Do that shaped robot personhood law. She initially worked as an industrial yard worker, maintaining Midwestlander military craft — jets, tanks, and other vehicles — skilled, technical, practical work that suited her preference for finding security through mastery of a useful craft. Outside of work, she developed a deep love for Steampunk and began creating her own art in that style — a private interior world she built for herself.

As anti-robot sentiment rose across Midwestland, she was let go from her industrial position, and by force of circumstance rather than desire, she became a leader. She did not want to lead, did not want to be depended upon, and did not want her private reserves of energy and attention drained by the needs of others — but her sense of loyalty and duty made her the person her community turned to, and she could not refuse. She led her fellow robots out of primarily human cities in Midwestland and the Colorado Republic, toward the establishment of their own separate, parallel communities — a pragmatic resettlement rather than a confrontation.

That quiet resettlement effort was already underway when the War of Upper Earth broke out — a war she did not cause and had no part in starting. The war's escalating anti-robot violence forced her small effort to become something much larger: the communities she had organized became a refuge, then a rallying point, for robots displaced by the fighting. She did not seek a wartime command role, but by the war's end she was a recognized, credible leader among the robot population at large, not just her own original community.

This is precisely why, once the war was won, Upper Earth's governments (the Assembly Powers) identified her specifically and maneuvered her into signing the Falkland Treaty on behalf of the defeated robot population — she was "invited" to "volunteer," the kind of invitation that isn't really one. A credible robot leader's signature made the exile look consented-to rather than simply imposed by the victors. She signed at the city of Stanley, Falkland Islands, negotiating the exile of the robot population (and their human allies) from a position of certain defeat.

Following the signing, she is presumed to have first taken up residence in Rothera before relocating permanently to Byrd — the city built entirely underground, whose vast, isolated, largely private scale, paired with a genuine sense of shared community despite that isolation, suits her well after her one great unwilling act of public leadership.

## Role in Main Story / World
- Connection to larger narrative: Represents a chapter of pre-exile robot history often overshadowed by the larger narrative of the War of Upper Earth — someone who tried to find a third option (not fighting, not submitting, but quietly leaving and building something separate), and who was ultimately made the human-facing signatory of the treaty that formalized the robot population's exile, against her own preference to simply be left alone.
