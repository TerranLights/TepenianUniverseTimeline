# The First Interwar Period — Timeline (2083–2564)

*(Renamed 2026-07-05 — this era was originally just "The Interwar Era," but the developer identified a second, later Tepenian span (2564–2812, Falkland Treaty to Long Night War) that also fits "interwar" — the gap between the 2083 global nuclear war and the 2564 War of Upper Earth on one side, and the gap between that same war's aftermath and the next war 248 years later on the other. This era is now "The First Interwar Period"; the 2564–2812 span is "The Second Interwar Period" (see `Worldspace/Locations-and-Levels/Outside-World/Tepenian-Federation/` for that timeline's own scaffolding, and every file previously using bare "interwar" to mean 2564–2812 has been updated to say "Second Interwar" explicitly).*

The span between the global nuclear war of September 26, 2083 and the signing of the
Falkland Treaty on June 21, 2564 — roughly **480.7 years**. This document codifies that
era using a composite story-beat structure fusing four sources, each brought in for the
specific purpose/objective it serves — not one primary framework with the others
layered on top *(corrected 2026-07-05 — this section previously miscounted "three
sources" while listing four, and described Save the Cat as "the primary/main source";
the developer clarified all four were fused deliberately, based on what each was
needed for)*:

- **Blake Snyder, *Save the Cat*** — standard beat names and the recommended duration
  percentages, the baseline structural skeleton the other sources attach to. Drawn
  from as fully as possible.
- **James Scott Bell, *Plot and Structure*** — the "Doorway of No Return" framing for
  the two act breaks, the "Mirror Moment" for the midpoint, and supplementary beats
  (Kick in the Shins, Care Package, Q-Factor, Pet the Dog — not yet dated). Drawn
  from as fully as possible.
- **John Truby, *The Anatomy of Story*** — character-web concepts (Ally/Allies,
  Opposition, Fake Ally, Moral Decision, etc. — none yet dated/placed as of
  2026-07-05, but intended to be used as fully as possible, same as Save the Cat/Bell).
- **Joseph Campbell, *The Hero's Journey*** — used selectively, not comprehensively
  (clarified 2026-07-05): a Campbell beat is kept only if it can be reinterpreted into
  sci-fi form. Cross-referenced so far where its terminology overlaps Save the Cat's
  (Road of Trials ≈ "Fun and Games" / "Promise of the Premise"; Refusal of the Call ≈
  "Debate"); intended future additions include Apotheosis, Atonement with the Father,
  and Meeting with the Goddess (none yet dated/placed). Beats that are irreducibly
  fantasy-coded and don't reinterpret into sci-fi — e.g. **Magic Flight** (initially
  misnamed "Magical Boon" in conversation, corrected 2026-07-05) — are deliberately
  left out. This is the one source used selectively by reinterpretability, not pulled
  from as comprehensively as the other three.

**Important caveat:** the in-world dates below were chosen for what actually happens
in Tepenian/Upper Earth history at each point — they were *not* forced to match Save
the Cat's recommended percentages exactly. They land close (see the percentage column
below) but are not required to be exact, and should not be adjusted purely to chase a
rounder percentage.

Many beats are currently **structural placeholders** — the date/span is fixed, but the
actual historical event that occupies it is undeveloped. These are marked TBD and are
open for development in future sessions.

## Beat-by-Beat Detail

### Opening Image
**Sept 26, 2083 – ~June 2088**

The global nuclear war. **Confirmed canon** (see `World_History_Reference.md`): a
global nuclear war permanently reshaped the world on September 26, 2083 — countries
collapsed, borders were redrawn, the pre-war geopolitical order was destroyed. In the
same year, the first fully sentient, fully conscious robot, **En.Ki.Du**, was created
at U.R.U.K. (the Underwater Research Union: Kappa), off the coast of the Sovereign
Republic of Hawaii — both an Epic of Gilgamesh reference (Enkidu; Uruk) and its own
acronym: **Entelechian Kinetic Duumvir** (Aristotle's entelechy; moves under his own
power; one half of a two-person team with his human counterpart Gil(bert) Gamesh,
not yet otherwise documented). He is the prototype from which all subsequent Doll
manufacturing diverged, and the only Doll that is noticeably robotic rather than
externally human-looking. The conjunction is not confirmed as causal, but both
threads — the old world's end and the new order's beginning — start here.

**TBD:** what specifically unfolds through the immediate aftermath, up to ~June 2088.

**Source definition, Snyder's Opening Image (recorded 2026-07-05):** the first
visual/scene of the story — a thematic or grabbing image, scene, or short sequence
that establishes tone, mood, and scope. Functions as the "before" snapshot of the
hero (or world) that will have transformed by the story's end.

### Theme Stated
**September 2107**

TBD — no event yet established.

**Source definition, Snyder's Theme Stated (recorded 2026-07-05):** at ~5% — halfway
into Set-Up — another character directly, though not blatantly, instructs the
protagonist in their own psychological need, delivered in the form of a localized,
specific context rather than as an abstract statement of theme. Example given by the
developer: a child wants a brand-new bicycle, and wants it immediately; at the 5%
mark, the child's father tells him "you need to be patient" — a concrete, situational
line that Act 3 later turns out to have been about all along (the child learning the
value of patience).

### Set-Up
**~June 2088 – ~July 2131**

TBD in detail, but the roster of post-2083 successor nations is confirmed (migrated
2026-07-12): Unified Korea, the Sinian Federation, the Sovereign Republic of Hawaii,
Midwestland, the Colorado Republic, the People's Democratic Republic of Cascadia,
the Appalachia Republic, the Prairie Federation, the Republic of Sonora, the
Republic of Alaska, the People's Democratic Republic of New England, and the CSA
(Texas — flagged in source material for a possible rename to "Dixieland"). See
`World_History_Reference.md` and the "New North America" map at
`Reference-Images/Maps/North America with tentative labels.jpg`. No specific
founding event or date within this span is established yet — the Sinian
Federation's formation is explicitly deferred, and Unified Korea's reunification
date remains TBD.

**Placement rule (2026-07-05):** Bell's **Care Package** lands somewhere within
Set-Up — not dated to a specific point yet, but structurally anchored here, in the
first half of Act 1.

**Source definition, Bell's Care Package (recorded 2026-07-05):** some form of gift
given to the protagonist — not a literal "birthday present" or "Christmas present,"
but a form of goodwill. Can be physical, verbal, psychological, or "actional"
(something someone does). This is the setup half of a payoff pair with Bell's
**Q-Factor** (see Dark Night of the Soul, below) — the developer explicitly builds
these two together.

### Catalyst
**July 2131**

TBD — no event yet established.

**Source definition, Snyder's Catalyst (recorded 2026-07-05):** the actual triggering
event. Equally valid alternate term: Bell's **Disturbance**. Cross-references
directly to Campbell's **Call to Adventure**. Important craft rule, per the
developer: this is the *one and only* event in the entire story allowed to be
random/coincidental — every other event must follow causally from what came before.
Because it's the sole exception, it's especially important that this
particular event actually matters.

### Debate
**~July 2131 – ~November 2179**

TBD. Cross-referenced as Campbell's "Refusal of the Call" and Bell's "Argument Against
Transformation" — thematically, this should be the period where the story's central
tension (robot personhood? robot-human coexistence?) is being resisted or argued
before the world commits to change.

**Source definition, Snyder's Debate (recorded 2026-07-05):** the hero's hesitation
before committing to the journey — "Can I face this challenge? Do I have what it
takes? Should I go at all?" The last chance to say no. Confirmed equivalent, per the
developer: Bell's **Argument Against Transformation** and Campbell's **Refusal of the
Call** are the same beat under different names, not merely thematically similar.

### Break into Two
**~January 2180** *(month set; year open to adjustment)*

TBD — the "Doorway of No Return #1." Whatever happens here commits the era's
trajectory toward the Jeju-do ruling at the Midpoint.

**Source definition, Snyder's Break into Two (recorded 2026-07-05):** the hero
commits and enters the new world of the story — literally (leaves home, takes the
case, joins the mission) or internally (an irreversible decision, accepting a truth).
Should read as a door closing behind them; the old rules no longer apply. Confirmed
equivalent, per the developer: this **is** Bell's **Doorway of No Return #1**, not
just a parallel concept — the "point of no return" into Act 2.

### B-Story Trigger(s) *(multiple)*
**TBD — early portion of the Road of Trials interval**

Not yet dated.

**Source definition, Snyder's B-Story (recorded 2026-07-05):** a parallel plot — a
separate plotline that *seemingly* has no connection whatsoever to the main story,
which then converges with the main story at the **Midpoint / Mirror Moment**. The
"seemingly unrelated" quality is load-bearing: the B-Story's disconnection from the
A-plot must read as genuine to the audience right up until the convergence, not be
telegraphed early.

### Kick in the Shins
**TBD — early portion of the Road of Trials interval**

Bell's term for an early jolt that raises stakes shortly after Break into Two. Not yet
dated.

**Source definition, Bell's Kick in the Shins (recorded 2026-07-05):** an event of
things going wrong for the protagonist, early in Act 2. Unlike Snyder, Bell gives no
exact percentage marker for this — only the instruction that it should happen early
in Act 2, on the reasoning that "you shouldn't let things stay trouble-free for very
long." It's a complication that occurs early in Road of Trials, must be internally
consistent with the surrounding world/environment, and must be relevant to the plot
(not a random, disconnected setback).

### Road of Trials / Fun and Games / Promise of the Premise
**~November 2179 – ~2317**

TBD in detail, but this ~138-year span is the era's longest single beat — the bulk of
Act Two-A. Somewhere within it, Gyeong-ja Yun's case (the robot nightclub DJ whose
Mafioso boss exploited her labor) must occur, since it triggers the Jeju-do ruling
that closes this span (see Midpoint, below).

**Source definition, Snyder's Fun and Games / Promise of the Premise (recorded
2026-07-05):** the part of the story that delivers on the premise's hook — whatever
made the audience want this story in the first place actually gets experienced here
(the magic school, the investigation, the romance). Confirmed equivalent, per the
developer: this **is** Campbell's **Road of Trials**, not merely a parallel or
overlapping concept — the two terms name the same beat.

### Midpoint / Mirror Moment
**April 27, 2318** — **Confirmed canon**

The International Court of Diplomacy at Jeju-do rules that robots have full legal
personhood, following the case of Gyeong-ja Yun (see `World_History_Reference.md`).
This is the era's structural and historical fulcrum: the moment robots gain the legal
standing that defines the following 246 years, up to the Falkland Treaty.

**Source definitions (recorded 2026-07-05, for reference and reuse when scaffolding
the Second Interwar Period):**
- **Blake Snyder, Midpoint** — either a **False Victory** or a **False Defeat**.
- **J.S. Bell, Mirror Moment** — either (A) the protagonist confronts three
  realization-questions: *Who am I? What have I become? What must I become?* — or
  (B) the protagonist realizes *"I'm probably going to die,"* where "death" can be
  one or more of: **physical** (literal death), **social** (ostracism, loss of
  reputation/respect, a romantic partner leaving), **professional** (losing a job
  they're committed to), or **psychological** (realizing a belief/belief-system they
  held was not just wrong but falsely life-supporting).
- Campbell's Hero's Journey has no clearly identified equivalent "middle" beat
  (per the developer, 2026-07-05) — no cross-reference attempted here.

*Which specific sub-type (False Victory vs. False Defeat; realization-questions vs.
"probably going to die," and if the latter, which kind of death) applies to the
Jeju-do ruling above is not yet decided.*

**Placement rule (2026-07-05):** Bell's **Pet the Dog** beat must land close to the
Midpoint — either just before or just after it, not floating freely — though its own
specific event here is not yet identified.

**Source definition, J.S. Bell's Pet the Dog (recorded 2026-07-05):** the protagonist
temporarily departs/sidesteps from the main plot to help another character who is
weaker than themselves. This action must have an effect (direct or indirect) on the
progression of the rest of the plot, and must contribute somehow to the course of
events leading up to the triggering of Act 3.

### Bad Guys Close In
**~March 2318 – ~2444**

TBD in detail, but this span now has two confirmed anchor points within it:
- **Hana Jinn** — requested by name to A.R.U.Ta.G. shortly after the Jeju-do ruling
  (early-to-mid 2300s) to research metamaterials for orbital mass drivers. See
  `Worldspace/Characters/Dolls/Past_History_-_Known_to_Tepenians/Hana Jinn/`.
- **Mallory Dufay** — oversaw the actual first orbital launches and construction,
  placed closer to the mid-2300s. See
  `Worldspace/Characters/Dolls/Past_History_-_Known_to_Tepenians/Mallory Dufay/`.

**Reconciled 2026-07-12:** the "rising pressure" this beat name implies runs in
parallel with, not in place of, the Hana/Mallory orbital-optimism thread — both are
true at once across this span. Legal personhood (won at the Midpoint, 2318) never
translated into being treated as though it were real: Ji-Eun Kim's own pre-war
history establishes "systematic discrimination, professional exclusion, and social
hostility" toward robots in Unified Korea as an ongoing condition throughout this
period, not a single event, despite personhood being legally settled (see
`Worldspace/Characters/Dolls/Ji-Eun Kim/`). Maggie Aarden's own pre-war history
shows the same pattern in Midwestland: she's pushed out of her industrial job
specifically "as anti-robot sentiment began rising across the world" (see
`Worldspace/Characters/Dolls/Maggie Aarden/`). Neither event is dated precisely
within 2318–2444, but both confirm the antagonistic pressure as a slow-building
undercurrent across the whole span, with the sharpest spike concentrated in the
years immediately preceding the War of Upper Earth (~2500s) — i.e., toward this
beat's tail end or into the Finale, not evenly spread across it.

**Source definition, Snyder's Bad Guys Close In (recorded 2026-07-05):** the second
half of Act 2 — obstacles compound and become overwhelming, the protagonist's usual
strengths stop working, plans get foiled, allies/teams may fracture. Pressure builds
on both an external axis (literal antagonists, rivals, systems, deadlines) and an
internal/psychological axis (the protagonist's own flaws finally catching up with
them) simultaneously — widely considered the hardest beat to write well.

### All Is Lost
**~2444**

TBD — no event yet established. Structurally, this should be the era's low point.

**Source definition, Snyder's All Is Lost (recorded 2026-07-05):** the low point —
the protagonist's situation collapses, no hope visible. Traditionally paired with a
**"whiff of death"**: a real or symbolic death (a mentor, an object, an idea) signaling
that the old self/old way of thinking is dying, bridging into Dark Night of the Soul.

**Placement rule (2026-07-05):** Truby's **Self-Revelation** can be placed anywhere
along the *entire second half of Act 2* (i.e., anywhere from the Midpoint at ~48.8%
through Break into Three at ~80.0%) — it is not fixed to All Is Lost specifically.
The developer's personal default preference is to place it here, at All Is Lost, but
explicitly not as a hard rule: if a given story's own logic calls for Self-Revelation
to land somewhere else within that second-half-of-Act-2 range, it should go where it
actually fits rather than being forced into All Is Lost for consistency's own sake.

**Source definition, Truby's Self-Revelation (recorded 2026-07-05):** the hero has a
major revelation about who they really are, and grows as a person as a result —
unless the revelation is so painful it destroys them instead. Two variants: a
**psychological** self-revelation (stripping away a self-facade, seeing themselves
honestly for the first time) or a **moral** self-revelation (realizing they've been
wrong and have hurt others, and must change). Structurally, Self-Revelation is the
end-point of the same arc that "Need" begins back in Act 1.

### Dark Night of the Soul
**~March 2444 – ~June 2468**

TBD — the aftermath of the low point, leading into the final act break.

**Source definition, Snyder's Dark Night of the Soul (recorded 2026-07-05):** the
darkness before the dawn — the hero wallows in absolute hopelessness, beaten and
*knowing* it, before picking themself up and digging deep for a solution. Often the
point where the story's theme reflects most directly back at the protagonist. Can be
very brief — as short as a single beat/moment.

**Placement rule (2026-07-05):** both Truby's **Moral Decision** and Bell's
**Q-Factor** land somewhere within this span — neither dated to a specific point
within it yet, but both belong here structurally, not in an adjacent beat.

**Source definition, Truby's Moral Decision (recorded 2026-07-05):** the protagonist
is forced to choose between two (or more) equally difficult, equally plausible moral
choices — and the choice is constructed so that it's directly related to the
protagonist's own **Moral/Character Flaw**, which is established back in Act 1's
Setup (the first 10% of the era, leading up to the Catalyst at ~10%). The developer's
personal methodology explicitly pairs Moral Decision with Truby's own
**Self-Revelation** (see All Is Lost, above) and Bell's **Q-Factor** — all three are
used together, with Moral Decision and Q-Factor both placed within Dark Night of the
Soul specifically.

**Source definition, Bell's Q-Factor (recorded 2026-07-05):** the payoff half of the
Care Package/Q-Factor pair (see Set-Up, above). During a period of great strife and
hardship, the protagonist remembers that they received the Care Package — physically,
verbally, through something in the environment, or simply through memory. Acting on
this renewed mindset, the protagonist becomes able to act decisively.

### Break into Three
**~June 2468** *(month set; year open to adjustment)*

TBD — the "Doorway of No Return #2." Whatever happens here commits the era
irreversibly toward the War of Upper Earth.

**Source definition, Snyder's Break into Three (recorded 2026-07-05):** the moment of
clarity — the hero finally understands what they need to do, and more importantly,
who they need to become to do it. This is where the A-story and B-story intertwine to
help the hero find the solution; often the B-story's own lesson "clicks into place"
here, giving the hero what they need to try again. Confirmed equivalent, per the
developer: this **is** Bell's **Doorway of No Return #2**, not just a parallel
concept — the "point of no return" into Act 3.

### Finale
**~June 2468 – June 21, 2564**

TBD in detail, but this ~96-year span must terminate in the War of Upper Earth
itself (~2563–2564 — see `World_History_Reference.md`), the first anti-robot war,
driven by anti-robot sentiment reaching an all-time high in the years immediately
preceding it. Real developed material now exists for this span (migrated
2026-07-12), though not yet woven into a single connected narrative:

- **The Sinian Federation is confirmed as one of the Upper Earth powers that drove
  the war** — "The Sinian Federation was among the Upper Earth nations that
  persecuted robots and drove the War of Upper Earth" (source: InnerTepeniaGDD's
  `Local_Cultures/Mirny_Subnet/Zhongshan.md`). This directly answers part of
  `World_History_Reference.md`'s open "which nations or power blocs were the
  primary combatants" question — other primary drivers are still TBD.
- **Maggie Aarden** — a Midwestland industrial worker pushed out of her job as
  anti-robot sentiment rose. She organized a "third option": resettling robots out
  of primarily human cities into separate communities in Midwestland and the
  Colorado Republic. This resettlement effort was already underway when the War of
  Upper Earth broke out; escalating anti-robot violence turned it into a refuge,
  then a rallying point, for robots displaced by the fighting. Her resulting
  wartime leadership credibility is exactly why the victorious Assembly Powers
  maneuvered her into becoming the Falkland Treaty's robot signatory afterward — to
  make the exile look agreed-to rather than simply imposed. See
  `Worldspace/Characters/Dolls/Maggie Aarden/`.
- **Ji-Eun Kim** — anti-robot sentiment in Unified Korea specifically reached an
  all-time high in the years immediately preceding the war; she became one of the
  greatest nanotechnicians on the planet despite "systematic discrimination,
  professional exclusion, and social hostility." See
  `Worldspace/Characters/Dolls/Ji-Eun Kim/`.
- **Trisha Miller** — a Midwestland radio DJ robot; her own history records
  "human-robot tensions during her Midwestland years," opposition specifically for
  being a robot, alongside a loyal fan base. See
  `Worldspace/Characters/Dolls/Trisha Miller/`.

Not yet resolved: how these four threads connect to each other and to whatever
specific event constitutes this beat's own Break into Three (~June 2468) and the
War of Upper Earth's actual outbreak (~2563–2564).

**Source definition, Snyder's Finale — the "Five-Point Finale" / "Storming the
Castle" (recorded 2026-07-05):** the final ~20% of the story, broken into five
sequential steps: (1) **Gather the Team**, (2) **Storm the Castle**, (3) **High-Tower
Surprise**, (4) **Dig Down Deep**, (5) **Executing the New Plan**. Applies across
genres, not just action/adventure — every finale shares the common thread of pursuing
a concrete goal.

### END — Falkland Treaty
**June 21, 2564** — **Confirmed canon**

The War of Upper Earth ends. Upper Earth wins; the anti-robot side prevails. The
Falkland Treaty is signed, exiling robots (and their human allies) to Antarctica and
establishing the Federation of Tepenia. See `World_History_Reference.md` for full
detail.

## Character-Web Concepts (Truby) — Definitions

Unlike the Save the Cat/Bell beats above, Truby's character-web concepts aren't tied
to a specific point on the era's percentage timeline — they describe character
*relationships and functions*, applicable wherever the relevant characters interact.
Recorded here as they're defined, for reuse in both this timeline and the Second
Interwar Period's.

### Fake Ally
**Recorded 2026-07-05.** Does **not** mean "traitor" or "backstabber." A Fake Ally is
a character established as an ally to the protagonist in Act 1, who later acts
*against* the protagonist — but does so in a way fully aligned with the Fake Ally's
own belief system, goals, objectives, hesitations, fears, and principles, not out of
betrayal. Example given by the developer: the protagonist wants to eat at a
spicy-food restaurant; their ally has a severe intestinal condition that makes spicy
food unbearably painful, and steers them toward a non-spicy restaurant instead. The
ally never betrays or backstabs the protagonist at any point — they're simply acting
in their own genuine interest, which happens to conflict with the protagonist's.

### Ally / Allies
**Recorded 2026-07-05.** Self-explanatory, per the developer: the ally (or allies) to
the protagonist.

### Opposition
**Recorded 2026-07-05.** Self-explanatory, per the developer: whatever/whoever
constitutes the opposition to the protagonist.

## Campbell Concepts — Definitions, Placement Undetermined

These three Hero's Journey beats were identified 2026-07-05 as intended future
additions (reinterpreted into sci-fi form, per this file's source-list rule above),
but — per the developer — it's not yet known where along the era's percentage
timeline any of them belong. Unlike the Save the Cat/Bell beats, which have fixed or
rule-based placements, these may simply be flexible, applicable wherever they fit a
given story's specific events, rather than anchored to one percentage range. Recorded
here for reuse, without a placement claim.

### Meeting with the Goddess
**Recorded 2026-07-05.** The hero encounters a powerful embodiment of love or
goodness, and must earn a genuine connection with this figure. Through it, the hero
discovers how boundless and powerful love and unconditional surrender can be.

### Atonement with the Father
**Recorded 2026-07-05.** The hero confronts and is initiated by whatever holds
ultimate power in their life — often a father figure or a god-like/authority figure —
and must reconcile with that power to move forward. Frequently involves the hero
realizing this figure is mortal and flawed, and may have to rely on this imperfect
being for help regardless.

### Apotheosis
